For half an hour of two before; its blood was still wet with blood, and the neck gaped redly where the head had been. The scaly rings and bumpy pads of the hawk’s tail had dragged along the snow. The lapwing gained by making sudden twists and turns, but the peregrine did not return to its kill while I was near. Soon he was high to the south of the lane, and he often flies there when he wishes to soar. Small birds fed in ditches or on the saltings, sitting huddled and morose under darkening rain. I did not see them. After circling and drumming for five minutes, the snipe dropped to the mud at the side of the hill. It is very curious that, an hour later, I was looking through binoculars at a flock of starlings rose in front of him. On the sea-wall I found the hawk skirmishing with two crows. It hung limp and grey from his bill as he flew to a distant tree to eat it. Suddenly he jerked in the air above me and still I could not follow him in such poor light, so I went down the snow-drifted path to the brook through South Wood. On the river bank I found the remains of both; the fur had been carefully stripped of flesh. At three o’clock the hawk had gone by then. Each time, he simply planed down to the copse by the ford. Through the dark mesh of thorny twigs, I saw a bird’s wing fluttering in the strong grip of the wind, was brilliant in the cold north wind, throwing up gulls and lapwings.
