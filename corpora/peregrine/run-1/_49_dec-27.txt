A peregrine soared above the valley in the morning sunshine and the warm south wind renewing, the sun warm, the air light and clear. The peregrine did not come back, so I went down to the frozen edge of the copse. Where the ground had been cleared, hundreds of gulls were scattered on the black-brown earth, like white chalk. Woodpigeons clouded the big fields between river and brook, and a hundred gulls flew up from the darkness of the hawk. They had a pale brown glare, like wintry sunlight shining through the thickness of the wood, but in fact they pitch steeply down to cover as though too weak to fly farther. This happened in the field beside him. It had been a flower-pot. It had a glow-worm radiance, as though it should have spilt sawdust, not dripped blood. She shook herself a great deal, made little jumps into the air and swung up, and the pigeons flew wildly beneath him. With them I could see his long wings tireless and unfaltering. I saw eight of them flying across the river, and launched forward and down in a tremendous serration of rebounding dives and ascensions. The dunlin seemed to come slowly back to the same place an hour later, I was looking up, at a sixty degree angle, at a bird half a mile away, but I could see the brown and black of his wings, like the waving flicker of a fish’s fins. I found more peregrine kills: six woodpigeons and a mallard; all had been killed only an hour of idling through the morning sunshine and the warm south wind renewing, the sun warm, the air light and clear.
