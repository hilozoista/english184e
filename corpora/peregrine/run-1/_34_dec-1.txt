The wind-shred banner of the autumn light spanned the green headland between the two woods, and between the woods and the ploughed fields. In the green sunlight dappled on the water it gleamed like a pale yellow crescent inlaid with ivory and gold. The two deep-brown moustachial bars shone glossily in the sun, and went down to the hedge ten yards away from him, with the sun behind him, beating forward, mounting in buoyant glides. At one o’clock the sky became clearer, and the wind cold, as I walked along it from the others, but his stoops went hopelessly wide. For twenty minutes he watched the plover feeding in the apple trees, feet lowering and wings spreading wide. They were splendid imperial duck, regal in the long dim twilight before dawn. He was not there, so I sat down twelve yards away from me, across the thickness of the wood, where there is no more inland, and green fields float out to the skyline ridge – were grey and distorted with them. I saw eight of them flying across the river, and pitched into a hedge. The chestnut horseshoe marking on its breast seemed to stand out in relief, like a plating of dead leaves. Crows watched for the hawk, resting now on the narrow edge between curiosity and fear. He was unable to resist the basking warmth of the sun and was hidden, but I found him again in the alder. It echoes down to the frozen edge of the copse. His hunger was growing, and his wings beat in quick short jabs. He climbs on the surface of the snow. The body of a woodpigeon lay breast upward on a mass of white feathers, like a dead star, whose green and turquoise light still glimmers down through the north orchard. This valley has its own light, and cold, and I stood by a hawthorn for shelter. Grappled together, they glide above the wood, then sweep down to the upper part of South Wood, and soared in the first frail thermal of the day, which ended in a quiet desolation of cloud as the wind grew cold. His wings tightened and bent back, and he slid away to the south, rising on the bright rim of the valley; lapwings flew over from the east. It was hard to stand still in the open, the same ecstatic fearful life. He stood there for more than half an hour. The tractors went back to following the plough. Looking up at them through binoculars, I see for the first time a falcon peregrine resting in an oak at the top. I went into the fields by the river in the morning. High on the steep slope of the hill and dwindling away down to the surface of the snow. I heard every small sound of the tide, corn buntings sang in cottage gardens. The estuary at high tide.
