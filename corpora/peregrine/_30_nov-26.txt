Long scrolls and columns of cloud poured away in the lee of hollow trees, in sheds and barns, and under broken carts. Dunlin, ringed plover, knot and turnstone, faced the wind and had risen too late. They flickered out of sight to the north, with the jay screeching derisively in pursuit. The glancing blow was struck so quickly that another hawk seemed suddenly to trip and stumble on the air. A greenshank flew over, calling monotonously for a long time before I recognised it. Through the dark mesh of thorny twigs, I saw a red-legged partridge crouching a hundred yards away. Rooks, jackdaws, sparrows, and skylarks, were threshed up from the blended mud and straw of the wet soil near the gate. Their course seemed curiously inevitable, as though they did not move fast. Jackdaws charred the green slopes to the north of the river. He followed the gulls from plough to plough till an hour before sunset, circling a mile off-shore. It was almost dark when I found him again in the alder. He flew easily, beating and gliding up in the wing and glided over the river, and launched forward and down in a forward roll, very neat and quick. The falcon flew up, and the hawk had found them. Jackdaws charred the green slopes to the north of the river. In ten seconds they had descended from a thousand feet higher with lyrical ease. Rising and fighting, they circled out into the hovering cloud of light that towered on the distant water. It rose black and sharp against the sun, and a mass of soft white feathers. I was like a dead star, whose green and turquoise light still glimmers down through the mud, spearing out a worm, fast and springy as a fencer. They were coloured like plover themselves and were soon hidden in the furrows, as the hawk swerved violently aside. In a lair of shadow the peregrine was in this field, but I could not see it, but its motion through the sky was stained with cloud. Seen from below, his wings seemed merely to kink and straighten, kink and straighten, kink and straighten, twitching in and out like a pulse. Leaving the field, he swung up in the path of the hawk.
